# Sample Queries & Thought Processes

Five realistic queries a movie-search user might ask, chosen to span different combinations of traits (lexical recall, mood/vibe, theme, narrative technique, metadata constraints, reception, franchise context). For each, I walk through how *I* would reason through the answer if a friend asked me — what I anchor on, what I rule out, and which titles I'd ultimately recommend with the rationale.

---

## Query 1 — Vague title recall
> "What's that movie where the guy can't form new memories and has tattoos all over his body to help him solve his wife's murder?"

**Trait mix:** plot_events (memory loss, tattoos, investigation) + viewer_experience (puzzle-y, disorienting) + lexical (none — the user *can't* name it; that's the whole point).

### Thought process
1. **Identify the load-bearing details.** Three concrete plot anchors: (a) anterograde amnesia / can't form new memories, (b) tattoos as a memory aid, (c) investigating a wife's murder. The tattoo + amnesia combo is unusually specific — it's almost a fingerprint.
2. **Rule out near-neighbors.** "Memory loss" alone pulls *50 First Dates*, *The Bourne Identity*, *Eternal Sunshine* — none of those involve tattoos as an external memory system. The tattoo detail is what disambiguates.
3. **Confirm with the structural detail.** The film I'm thinking of is also told in reverse — a separate hint that often surfaces alongside this query. If the user mentions "told backwards" later, that's confirmation, not contradiction.
4. **Sanity check the wife angle.** The protagonist's wife is the murder victim he's avenging. Matches.

### Top pick
- **Memento (2000, dir. Christopher Nolan)** — single confident answer. The amnesia + Polaroid/tattoo system + wife's murder is essentially unique to this film. No close runner-up worth listing; offering alternatives would just add noise.

## Query 2 — Mood + practical constraints for tonight
> "I want something light and feel-good but with actual substance, around 90 minutes, on a streaming service."

**Trait mix:** viewer_experience (warm, uplifting) + watch_context (weeknight, low-effort) + metadata (runtime ≤ ~100 min, streaming availability) + an implicit quality bar ("substance" = not throwaway).

### Thought process
1. **Decode "substance."** The user is gently pushing back against pure fluff. They want emotional weight or craft, just not heavy or sad. That rules out Hallmark-tier romcoms *and* anything melancholic dressed as feel-good (e.g., *Lost in Translation*).
2. **Decode "around 90 minutes."** Treat as a soft ceiling — 85–105 min is in-bounds. A 110-min film with great pacing is fine; a 90-min slog isn't.
3. **Walk the quadrants.**
   - *Warm + crafted + short:* animation does this well (Studio Ghibli, Pixar shorts compilations, *Paddington 2*).
   - *Comedy with heart:* indie dramedies often hit this — *Sing Street*, *The Big Sick* (slightly long), *Brooklyn*.
   - *Cozy mystery / hangout films:* *Knives Out* style, but most are 130+ min. Skip.
4. **Apply the streaming filter mentally.** I don't actually know real-time streaming windows, so I'd flag this as the one piece I genuinely can't verify and would suggest the user double-check on JustWatch.

### Top picks
- **Paddington 2 (2017)** — ~103 min, near-universal critical love (Rotten Tomatoes consensus is famously immaculate), genuinely warm without being saccharine. The "substance" bar is met by its craft, not its themes.
- **Sing Street (2016)** — ~106 min, Irish coming-of-age musical, earns its emotional beats. Hits "feel-good with substance" cleanly.
- **The Princess Bride (1987)** — ~98 min. A safe re-watchable pick if the user hasn't seen it; if they have, drop it.

I'd lead with *Paddington 2* because it has the lowest risk of misfiring on tone.

## Query 3 — Actor against type
> "What are the best dramatic performances from actors who are mostly known for comedy?"

**Trait mix:** lexical (specific actors) + reception (acclaim, awards) + a *meta*-trait (career-context contrast) that doesn't map to any single channel — it's something I have to reason about, not retrieve.

### Thought process
1. **Reframe what "known for comedy" means.** It implies a comedic baseline strong enough that a serious turn was a *surprise*. Robert De Niro doing drama isn't surprising; Adam Sandler doing drama is.
2. **Build the candidate actor list first, then map performances.** Bill Murray, Adam Sandler, Jim Carrey, Robin Williams, Steve Carell, Will Ferrell, Jonah Hill, Melissa McCarthy, Eddie Murphy, Jamie Foxx (debatable — already had range), Mo'Nique.
3. **For each, find the canonical "wait, *they* did that?" performance.** Filter for ones that landed critically (awards nominations or strong reviews), not just attempts.
4. **Rank by the *gap* between expectation and result.** Sandler in *Uncut Gems* is a bigger gap than Carell in *Foxcatcher* (Carell had already done *Little Miss Sunshine*). The bigger the whiplash, the better the answer.
5. **Avoid the trap of listing Robin Williams in every slot.** He had so many dramatic roles that "comedian going dramatic" almost stopped being a surprise. Pick one signature: *Good Will Hunting*.

### Top picks
- **Adam Sandler — *Uncut Gems* (2019)** — the highest variance pick on the list. Anxiety-attack performance, NYFCC Best Actor, Oscar snub became a meme.
- **Bill Murray — *Lost in Translation* (2003)** — Oscar-nominated, defined the late-career Murray reinvention.
- **Jim Carrey — *Eternal Sunshine of the Spotless Mind* (2004)** — restrained, melancholic, basically the inverse of his *Ace Ventura* persona.
- **Robin Williams — *Good Will Hunting* (1997)** — Best Supporting Actor Oscar; the canonical "dramatic comedian" reference point.
- **Mo'Nique — *Precious* (2009)** — Best Supporting Actor Oscar after a stand-up career.

I'd lead with the Sandler pick because it's the most recent and the contrast is the sharpest, so it's the one most likely to make the user say "oh yeah, I've been meaning to see that."

## Query 4 — Theme exploration
> "Films that deal with grief without being depressing."

**Trait mix:** plot_analysis (theme = grief, loss) + viewer_experience (tone = not depressing — bittersweet, hopeful, even funny) + narrative_techniques (often involves magical realism or comedy as a lens). No useful lexical or metadata constraints.

### Thought process
1. **Resolve the apparent contradiction.** "Grief without being depressing" = the film acknowledges loss but the dominant emotional register is something else: humor, wonder, warmth, catharsis, hope. The user probably wants to *feel* something, just not flattened by it.
2. **Categorize the strategies films use to do this.**
   - *Comedy as a coping lens:* the film grieves *while* being funny. (*The Big Sick*, *Death at a Funeral*.)
   - *Magical realism / fantasy frame:* metaphor softens the edges. (*A Monster Calls*, *Coco*.)
   - *Forward-motion narrative:* grief is the engine, but the story is about what happens *next*. (*About a Boy*, *We Bought a Zoo*.)
   - *Communal celebration:* grief processed through ritual or relationship. (*Coco* again, *Truly Madly Deeply*.)
3. **Disqualify the heavy hitters.** *Manchester by the Sea*, *Sophie's Choice*, *Three Colors: Blue* — all phenomenal, all violate the "not depressing" constraint. Set aside.
4. **Diversify the picks.** Pull one from each strategy so the user has options matching whatever specific tone they're in the mood for.

### Top picks
- **Coco (2017)** — magical realism + cultural celebration of remembrance. The clearest "grief becomes warmth" film I can name.
- **The Big Sick (2017)** — uses comedy and the messiness of new love to process potential loss. Funny first, moving second.
- **A Monster Calls (2016)** — bittersweet, fantasy-framed, lets the user *feel* it without sinking. The heaviest of the picks; flag that.
- **About a Boy (2002)** — grief is in the bones of the story (suicide, isolation), but the film itself is a warm comedy about found connection.

I'd lead with *Coco* because it's the lowest-risk match for the spirit of the request.

## Query 5 — Franchise + era constraint
> "Best superhero movies from the 2000s, before the MCU took over everything."

**Trait mix:** lexical (franchise/genre = superhero) + metadata (date range: roughly 2000–2008, since *Iron Man* is the cutoff) + reception (best). The "before the MCU" phrasing is doing real work — it implies the user wants the *pre-shared-universe* aesthetic: standalone, often darker, less formula-bound.

### Thought process
1. **Pin the date range precisely.** *Iron Man* released May 2008. Strictest reading: 2000–April 2008. Loose reading: 2000–2008 inclusive, since the MCU didn't really *dominate* until *The Avengers* (2012). I'd use the loose reading and note the edge.
2. **Inventory the era.** This was the Raimi *Spider-Man* trilogy, the original *X-Men* trilogy, the start of Nolan's Batman, the much-maligned *Daredevil*/*Elektra*/*Catwoman*/*Ghost Rider* run, and *Hellboy*. Pixar's *The Incredibles* counts if we're being inclusive.
3. **Apply "best" carefully.** Critical reception, cultural staying power, and how well they hold up today. *X2* and *Spider-Man 2* are the two consensus high points of the era; *The Dark Knight* (2008) is the obvious peak but is also the film that arguably *ended* the era it belongs to.
4. **Honor the spirit of the question.** The user wants pre-MCU energy: standalone stakes, director-driven tone, no post-credits scene homework. That biases me away from anything overtly franchise-building.

### Top picks
- **The Dark Knight (2008)** — the apex of the era, even if it's a borderline date-wise. Standalone, auteur-driven, redefined what the genre could be. Lead pick.
- **Spider-Man 2 (2004)** — for years the consensus "best superhero movie." Raimi at his peak; emotional core still holds up.
- **The Incredibles (2004)** — original superhero story, no comic baggage, holds up better than most live-action entries.
- **X2: X-Men United (2003)** — the high-water mark of the original Singer trilogy; ensemble done right.
- **Batman Begins (2005)** — sets up *The Dark Knight*, but works on its own as a grounded origin reboot.

I'd order them as above but flag that *The Dark Knight* is the top pick if the user only watches one, and *The Incredibles* if they want the most original take on the genre rather than the most acclaimed.

## Notes on what these queries reveal

- **Query 1** is a pure recall problem — one right answer, dominated by plot anchors.
- **Query 2** is multi-constraint with a fuzzy quality bar; the constraint I can't verify (streaming) needs to be flagged honestly rather than guessed.
- **Query 3** requires meta-reasoning the system can't retrieve directly — "career context" is something I synthesize, not search.
- **Query 4** is a tone-aware thematic search where the failure mode (depressing pick) is worse than a mediocre pick.
- **Query 5** depends on interpreting an *implicit* constraint ("before the MCU took over") that's narrower than its literal reading.

The common thread: the hardest part of every query is figuring out what the user actually wants behind the words, then ranking *appropriateness* — not just relevance.
